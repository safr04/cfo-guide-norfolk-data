About this guide

What this methodology does — and what it produces

The CFO Guide gives municipal finance offices a structured process for connecting what the city already spends, regulates, and controls to the spatial and environmental conditions it faces. The work is built from data the city already holds. The findings strengthen the capital plan, the credit narrative, grant competitiveness, and the quality of financial disclosure.

01 — Data Assembly
Three parallel data tracks
Parcel-level revenue base with hazard overlay. City-owned land inventory with exposure flags. Capital improvement plan as a geocoded project layer. Each track is built from publicly available data and produces a spreadsheet and map that feeds the methodology chapters.
02 — Methodologies
Nine exercises across three chapters
Chapter 1 measures property tax base exposure and runs revenue-at-risk scenarios. Chapter 2 classifies the CIP by hazard exposure and produces a mitigation inventory. Chapter 3 examines the city-owned land portfolio by hazard tier and revenue status.
03 — Norfolk Test Case
All exercises run with real data
Every exercise in the three methodology chapters has been executed using Norfolk, Virginia public data. Results are shown with actual figures, data sources, coordination requirements, and honest documentation of what the public data cannot provide.

42 percent of Norfolk’s taxable base sits in a mapped flood zone

Step by step How the data was assembled
▾
  1. 1
    Pull the assessor tax roll
    Downloaded the FY26 property assessment file from Norfolk's open data portal. The file contains 74,311 raw records covering every parcel account in the city, including assessed value, property type, owner, and address.
  2. 2
    Deduplicate extension records
    The raw file has multiple rows per parcel for accessory structures. Records were deduplicated by keeping the primary extension (R01 or C01) for each parcel, and retaining secondary records only where they had a unique parcel ID indicating a separately assessed unit. This reduced the record count from 74,311 to 72,677.
    Summing all raw records without this step inflates citywide assessed value by approximately $43 billion.
  3. 3
    Download parcel boundary polygons
    Downloaded 67,321 parcel boundary polygons from Norfolk's GIS open data hub. Each polygon carries a parcel ID (LRSN) used to join to the assessment file. The LRSN field in the shapefile was stored as a float and required conversion to a string before joining.
  4. 4
    Download FIRM flood zones
    Downloaded 1,665 flood zone polygons from Norfolk's GIS server. Zones include AE and VE (100-year SFHA), 0.2 PCT Annual Chance (500-year), and X (minimal). The FEMA national REST endpoint was unreliable; Norfolk's own GIS server was used instead.
  5. 5
    Run the spatial join
    Each parcel polygon was intersected against the 100-year SFHA, 500-year, and VE zone layers using GeoPandas. Any parcel with at least partial overlap with a flood zone was flagged as exposed.
  6. 6
    Join assessment data to flood flags
    Flood exposure flags were joined back to the assessment file by parcel ID, producing one row per parcel with assessed value, property type, taxability status, and one column per hazard zone.

Assumptions Decisions made in the analysis
▾
  • Partial overlap counts as exposed. If any part of a parcel polygon intersects a flood zone, the entire parcel is flagged as exposed. No partial-area weighting was applied.
  • Assessed value used in place of taxable value. The public dataset does not include partial exemptions. Assessed value is the closest available proxy. Levy figures overstate actual collections by approximately 21%.
  • Primary extension record kept for deduplication. Where multiple rows share a parcel ID, the R01 or C01 record is kept. Secondary records are retained only where they carry a unique parcel ID indicating a separately assessed unit.
  • FIRM effective date accepted as-is. The Norfolk FIRM was not independently verified against any recent map amendment. Confirm the effective date before using this analysis for regulatory or disclosure purposes.
Missing elements What is not in the dataset
▾
  • Taxable value. Partial exemptions are not in the public dataset. True taxable value requires the finance office's internal tax roll.
  • Zoning layer. Available from Norfolk GIS. Not joined in this build. Would add one column per parcel without changing the hazard overlay.
  • Sea level rise projections. NOAA SLR inundation layers at multiple horizons were not applied. Only regulatory FIRM zones were used.
  • Heat and stormwater layers. Local stormwater failure areas and heat island data were not applied. Available from public works and NOAA; can be added as additional columns using the same spatial join process.
  • Approximately 4,654 unmatched parcel records. These did not match a parcel boundary polygon and were excluded. The matched records cover the large majority of citywide assessed value.

Methodologies

All nine exercises from Chapters 1, 2, and 3 completed with Norfolk data. Each exercise shows the method, the Norfolk result, the data used, the level of government coordination required, and what the public data cannot produce.

Chapter 1. Property Tax Base and Environmental Hazards

The property tax is the largest own-source revenue stream for most U.S. local governments and the most stable component of the operating budget. Standard revenue forecasting treats the geographic distribution of value as incidental. This chapter uses the parcel data file from Section 2A to put dollar and percentage figures on that exposure for Norfolk.

Three exercises are run: a present-day snapshot of the exposed tax base, a revenue-at-risk stress test under catastrophic and chronic scenarios, and a directional read on whether growth is concentrating in or out of hazard zones.

Input data Section 2A parcel file — 72,677 deduplicated records with assessed value, property type, and flood zone flags. Effective rate: $1.23 per $100. Actual FY25 levy: $328M.

Key constraint Public assessor data carries assessed value, not taxable value. Partial exemptions ($5.74B in Norfolk) are invisible. Levy figures are scaled to the actual budget to correct for this.

Exercise 2
Revenue at Risk Under Defined Scenarios
What permanent annual revenue loss would defined catastrophic and chronic scenarios produce?
▾
Method

Scenario A — Catastrophic: A major event causes a share of exposed parcels to reach the FEMA substantial damage threshold (50% value loss). Run at three severity levels: 15%, 30%, and 50% of exposed parcels affected.

Scenario B — Chronic: Flood exposure causes slow, persistent erosion of assessed value at 0.5%, 1.0%, and 1.5% per year, compounded over 10 years.

Compare each loss figure against the city's fiscal cushions: unrestricted general fund balance, annual debt service, and contingency reserves.

Exercise 3
Is the Tax Base Growing in Hazard Areas?
Is recent and pipeline development concentrating in or moving away from flood zones?
▾
Method

Pull building permits and certificates of occupancy (last 3–5 years) from the building department. Tag each record by flood zone using the 2A parcel file. Compare the share of new activity in hazard zones against the existing base share (42.3%). Use active and approved permits for the forward-looking pipeline. Note the dominant zoning category in hazard areas where activity is occurring.

Norfolk result
Building permit data not available as a public structured dataset. Norfolk's building permit records are not published on the open data portal. This exercise requires a direct data request to the Building Inspections Department or Planning. The exercise could not be completed from publicly available data alone.

As a proxy, the CIP program was used to assess forward-looking investment direction. CIP investment in flood zones is 9.0% of the five-year total, against a tax base exposure of 42.3%. This signals a ↓ downward direction — capital is being committed at a lower rate in flood zones than the existing base exposure would imply. However, this is an incomplete signal and should not substitute for the permit analysis.

Chapter 2. The Capital Improvements Plan and Environmental Hazards

A capital improvements plan is a financial commitment to specific locations. The exercises in this chapter use the CIP project layer from Section 2C to give the finance office three quantified findings: where capital is going relative to the hazard profile, whether reserves are sized to that exposure, and what the city is investing in to reduce its exposure.

Each project was classified with an exposure tier (0–3) and function flags (MA for mitigation, EF for essential facility, MA+EF for both) based on project name, description, location, and flood zone overlay.

Input data Section 2C CIP layer — 121 projects, $1.43B five-year, 67 geocoded, 108 with year-by-year schedules from the FY25 budget PDF.

Classification note Exposure tiers assigned from flood overlay. MA/EF flags assigned analytically from project names and descriptions. Working session with capital planning required to verify and finalize all classifications.

Chapter 3. City-Owned Land and Public Assets

Every city holds a land portfolio accumulated over decades. The fiscal question is what the land is doing for the city and what its current configuration implies for the revenue base, operating cost, and environmental exposure. This chapter uses the city-owned parcel inventory from Section 2B.

Three exercises: a portfolio-level exposure snapshot by hazard tier, a revenue-and-exposure cross-sort, and a directional read on underused parcels. Norfolk's portfolio analysis is constrained by the absence of the fixed-asset subledger and lease revenue records — both internal documents.

Input data Section 2B city-owned parcel inventory — 2,101 parcels filtered from the assessor file by owner field. Hazard overlay from the same flood zone layers as 2A.

Key constraint Assessor values are historical acquisition cost proxies, not replacement cost or market value. Revenue status requires internal lease records. Custody and current use requires departmental working sessions.
